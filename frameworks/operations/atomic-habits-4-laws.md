# 4 Laws of Behavior Change

> Habits compound. The system you build is the life you live. The 4
> laws are the design rules. Use them to engineer routines that
> stick · without willpower.

---

## The 4 laws (and their inverses)

```
TO BUILD a habit:           TO BREAK a habit:
1 · Make it OBVIOUS         · Make it INVISIBLE
2 · Make it ATTRACTIVE      · Make it UNATTRACTIVE
3 · Make it EASY            · Make it HARD
4 · Make it SATISFYING      · Make it UNSATISFYING
```

Use the building laws for routines you WANT (Monday Content Drop).
Use the breaking laws for habits you DON'T want (mid-day Twitter
scrolling).

---

## Law 1 · Make it OBVIOUS

The hardest part of any habit is REMEMBERING to do it.

**3 design moves:**

### a · Implementation intention

The format: **"I will [behavior] at [time] in [location]."**

- Bad: "I'll run the marketing engine sometime Monday."
- Good: "I will run /marketing-engine at 7:00am at my desk Monday."

Implementation intentions raise success rate by ~60%.

### b · Habit stacking

Format: **"After [existing habit], I will [new habit]."**

Stack the new behavior onto an existing automatic one.

- "After I pour my coffee, I will open Claude Code."
- "After I close my laptop Friday at 5pm, I will run /cash-engine."
- "After I finish my morning workout, I will check the morning brief."

### c · Environment design

Make the cue visible. Remove the friction of remembering.

- **Phone alarm** labeled "MARKETING DROP" at 7am Monday
- **Calendar block** every Monday 7-7:30am · titled "Content drop"
- **Desktop sticky note** with the prompts you'll paste
- **Browser bookmark bar** with one-click to Claude Code

The goal: make running the routine the path of least resistance.

---

## Law 2 · Make it ATTRACTIVE

Habits we want to do · we do. Habits we feel obligated to do · we
skip.

**3 design moves:**

### a · Temptation bundling

Format: **"I will only [thing I want to do] WHILE [habit I need to do]."**

- "I'll only listen to my favorite podcast WHILE running /sales-engine."
- "I'll only have my fancy coffee WHILE doing my morning brief."

Pair the chore with the reward. Habit becomes attractive.

### b · Join a culture where the habit is normal

You become the average of your environment. If everyone in your
cohort runs their Monday Content Drop · you will too. Cohorts are a
feature, not a bug, of accountability programs.

### c · Reframe identity

Don't say "I have to run the engine." Say "I'm the kind of founder
who runs systematically."

Identity-based habits stick 10x better than outcome-based ones. (See
`leadership/identity-based-habits.md`.)

---

## Law 3 · Make it EASY

The biggest predictor of habit completion is the FRICTION between
you and the action.

**3 design moves:**

### a · The 2-minute rule

Make the habit take less than 2 minutes to start. Once started, it
extends naturally.

- Don't say "I'll write a newsletter every Wednesday." (60+ min)
- Say "I'll open the newsletter file every Wednesday at 8am." (30 sec)

The 30-second commitment leads to the 60-minute completion 80% of
the time. Without it · you procrastinate forever.

### b · Reduce decision count

Every decision is friction. Engineer them OUT.

- Pre-decide WHEN: Monday 7am
- Pre-decide WHERE: kitchen table
- Pre-decide WHAT: paste this exact prompt
- Pre-decide HOW LONG: 4 minutes max
- Pre-decide WHAT NEXT: review draft, hit save

You don't deliberate. You execute. The routine runs itself.

### c · Reduce activation energy

How many clicks/steps from "intend to do it" to "doing it"?

- 1 click: Excellent (one-touch alarm + paste)
- 3 clicks: OK
- 6+ clicks: dead

For each Purely Personal routine, audit:
1. How do I get to Claude Code? (≤ 1 click ideal)
2. How do I run the engine? (1 paste ideal)
3. How does the output get saved? (automatic ideal · no manual save)

## Law 4 · Make it SATISFYING

We repeat what feels good. We avoid what feels bad. Engineer the
satisfaction.

**3 design moves:**

### a · Track it (visible progress)

A streak counter, a checklist, a calendar grid. Crossing off the
day creates a small dopamine hit.

For Purely Personal:
- A "routines fired" tracker in Notion
- Weekly Friday-wrap shows: 6/6 routines fired (or 4/6)
- Monthly review shows: 24/24 weeks of streaks

### b · Immediate reward

Habits that pay off in 6 months are hard. Habits that pay off in 30
seconds stick.

- After /marketing-engine: 3 posts in your hand within 4 minutes
- After /sales-engine: 3 BANT-scored prospects within 6 minutes
- After /leadership-engine: a clear 60-second brief

The IMMEDIATE output IS the reward. Routine builds itself.

### c · Don't break the chain

Once you have a streak · the avoidance of breaking the chain becomes
its own motivation.

The first 3 weeks of any routine are the hardest. After 21
consecutive runs, the habit is mostly self-sustaining.

## The habit scorecard (audit existing habits)

For every habit you currently have:

```
1 · Is it OBVIOUS? (cue clear · environment supports it)
2 · Is it ATTRACTIVE? (do you actually want to do it?)
3 · Is it EASY? (low friction · minimal decisions)
4 · Is it SATISFYING? (immediate reward · tracking visible)
```

If any one is below 7/10 · the habit will erode. Fix that lever.

---

## How to break a bad habit (4 inverse laws)

For habits you want to STOP (mid-day Twitter, late-night work):

1 · **Make it INVISIBLE** · log out, delete the app, hide the
cookies
2 · **Make it UNATTRACTIVE** · note the cost (every Twitter scroll =
20 min lost from your real work)
3 · **Make it HARD** · use a website blocker · 30-second password ·
delete the bookmark
4 · **Make it UNSATISFYING** · pair with a small punishment ($1 to
charity per scroll · accountability partner)

---

## The chain rule (consistency wins over intensity)

> "Don't break the chain · 21 days, then 90, then forever."

A perfect run on Monday + miss Tuesday + perfect Wednesday is WORSE
than a mediocre Monday + mediocre Tuesday + mediocre Wednesday.

The chain matters more than perfection. Show up. Run the engine.
Even if the output isn't great. Even if you're tired.

After 90 days of unbroken chain, the routines become invisible to
you · they're just what Mondays ARE.

## Apply it to /operations-engine

When the engine generates the SOP for a new routine:

1. **Implementation intention** · pre-fill time + location
2. **Habit stack** · suggest pairing with existing automatic behavior
3. **Friction audit** · count clicks from intent to done · suggest
   reductions
4. **Tracker setup** · auto-create Notion tracker for streaks
5. **Reward identification** · what's the immediate satisfying output?

For onboarding new users:

1. Identify their existing automatic habits (when do they drink
   coffee? when do they open laptop?)
2. Stack the engine routines onto those existing habits
3. Pre-set alarms / calendar blocks
4. Pre-create the Notion tracker
5. First-week check-in: did the chain hold?

## The 1% rule (the long game)

A 1% improvement compounded daily = 37x in a year.
A 1% decline compounded daily = 0.03x in a year.

Every routine you DON'T break is a 1% deposit. Every routine you DO
break is a 1% withdrawal.

The math is brutal. The math is also forgiving. You don't have to
be perfect. You have to be 1% better today than yesterday.

The engines exist to make the 1% automatic.
