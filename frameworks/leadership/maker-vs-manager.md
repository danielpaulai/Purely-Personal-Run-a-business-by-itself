# The Maker Schedule · The Single Most-Misunderstood Calendar Move

> Most operators run their calendars on the manager schedule. Then
> wonder why no real work gets done. The maker schedule is what
> compounds. Both are valid · they just need to live on different
> days · or different blocks.

---

## The 2 schedules

### MANAGER SCHEDULE

The manager's day is broken into 1-hour blocks. Meetings fit
naturally. Each block is interruptible · because each task is
short.

A manager day looks like:
```
9 - 10am   · 1:1 with team member
10 - 11am  · Customer review meeting
11 - 12pm  · Strategy call
12 - 1pm   · Lunch
1 - 2pm    · Vendor meeting
2 - 3pm    · 1:1 with another team member
3 - 4pm    · Email batch
4 - 5pm    · Quick decisions / approvals
```

8 different things. None of them require deep focus. This is fine
for a pure manager.

### MAKER SCHEDULE

The maker's day is built around 4-hour deep work blocks. Interruption
DESTROYS productivity, because re-entering deep work takes 23
minutes.

A maker day looks like:
```
8am - 12pm  · Deep work block (writing · designing · building)
12 - 1pm    · Lunch + walk
1 - 2pm     · Email batch (the only time)
2 - 6pm     · Deep work block 2 (different project)
6pm         · Done
```

2 things. Long blocks. Deep focus. This produces 10x the work of
the manager day · for the same hours.

If you:
- Write code
- Design
- Write content (posts · newsletters · scripts)
- Build products
- Solve hard problems
- Make complex decisions
- Anything that requires "thinking it through"

You're a maker. The manager schedule will destroy your output.

Most knowledge workers (founders · solopreneurs · creators) are
makers · running on manager schedules · wondering why they feel busy
but ship little.

---

## The cost of breaking maker time

A 30-minute meeting at 11am DOESN'T cost 30 minutes. It costs the
ENTIRE morning.

Why:
- 8-9am · ramp into deep work (slowly)
- 9-11am · in flow · producing
- 11am meeting · OUT of flow
- 11:30am - 12pm · trying to ramp back · failing
- 12pm - lunch · gave up

A single mid-morning meeting can cost 4 hours of maker output.

This is why founders feel like they "didn't get anything done" · the
calendar SHOWED 4 hours of work time · but it was sliced by
meetings into useless fragments.

## How to design a maker week

For most operators · the goal is:
- 60-70% maker time
- 20-30% manager time
- 10% buffer / admin

### a · Theme days

Assign each day of the week a theme:

```
MONDAY    · STRATEGY day · 1 deep block + L10 meeting
TUESDAY   · MAKER day · 2 deep blocks · no meetings
WEDNESDAY · MAKER day · 2 deep blocks · no meetings
THURSDAY  · MEETINGS day · all customer/team meetings stack here
FRIDAY    · WRAP day · 1 deep block AM · admin + review PM
```

This gives you 2 pure maker days · 1 maker-heavy day · 1 meeting
day · 1 mixed day. 60% maker time.

### b · Block protection rules

```
1 · NO meetings before 1pm Monday-Wednesday
2 · NO email before 11am any day
3 · ONE meeting day per week (Thursday) · all stacked
4 · Friday afternoon · admin + reviews only
5 · Phone notifications OFF during deep blocks
```

These rules feel rigid. They're the only way maker time survives.

### c · The pre-block ritual (5 min)

Before every deep block:
- Phone face down · in another room
- Slack closed · email closed
- Water · coffee · everything you need at the desk
- ONE document open · the thing you're working on
- 90-min timer set

The ritual signals to your brain "deep mode now." Over time · it
triggers flow faster.

### d · The post-block ritual (5 min)

After every deep block:
- Save · save again · save to Drive
- Note ONE next step (where to pick up tomorrow)
- Stand up · walk · drink water
- THEN check email · Slack · phone

This separation prevents context-switching damage.

## The 23-minute reentry tax

When you switch contexts (deep work → check Slack → return to deep
work) · it takes 23 minutes to fully re-engage.

Math: 5 Slack checks/day = 5 × 23 = 115 minutes of cognitive
re-entry tax.

Eliminate Slack checks during deep blocks · recover ~2 hours/day
of useful capacity.

## When a manager schedule is actually correct

You SHOULD run a manager schedule when:
- You're in a pure operations role (running a team · not building)
- The day's value is COORDINATION not CREATION
- You have <2 hours of original creative work to do

Most days for most operators · there's 4+ hours of maker work that
deserves protection. Manager schedule defaults are wrong · default
should be maker.

## How to communicate maker time to others

People interrupt because they don't know your schedule. Tell them.

**With team:**
> "I'm in deep work blocks 9am-12pm and 1pm-4pm. Slack DMs during
> those times will be answered after the block. For urgent things
> · use [escalation channel]."

**With clients:**
> "I batch all client communications between 4-5pm Tuesdays and
> Thursdays. For anything truly urgent · email me with URGENT in
> the subject."

**With family:**
> "9-12 is my morning deep work. I'll be unavailable for chats. I'll
> see you for lunch."

Most people will respect the boundary IF you set one. Most operators
never set one · because they fear seeming "unavailable."

The truth: maker output is what makes you valuable. Protecting it is
not selfish · it's the job.

## The 1-question diagnostic

At the end of each day · ask:

> "Did I get a deep block today · or did I just do shallow work all
> day?"

If shallow · what broke the block? (Interruption? Meeting? Self?)
If deep · what protected it? (And how do I do that more?)

The answer over 30 days reveals your calendar's structural problem.

The makers ship. The managers schedule. Most operators try to be
both · and end up neither. The choice is to design the calendar
intentionally · or let interruptions design it for you.
