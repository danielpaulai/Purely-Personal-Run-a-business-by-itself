# StoryBrand Narrative · The 7-Part Story Framework

> The narrative spine underneath every brand that customers remember.
> 7 parts · in order · used everywhere from your homepage to your
> sales pitch to your "about" section.

The core insight: **the customer is the hero, not you.** You are the
guide. They have a problem. You have a plan. They take action. The
story has stakes — success and failure. You name both.

---

## The 7-part structure

```
1 · A CHARACTER ......... wants something
2 · HAS A PROBLEM ....... external · internal · philosophical
3 · MEETS A GUIDE ....... empathy + authority
4 · WHO GIVES A PLAN .... process or agreement
5 · CALLS THEM TO ACTION  direct · transitional
6 · SUCCESS ............. paint the after
7 · FAILURE ............. name what's at stake if they don't act
```

Every line of customer-facing copy answers one of these 7 questions.

---

## Part by part

### 1 · A character (the hero is your customer · NOT you)

What does your customer want? **One sentence.** Specific.

Wrong: "Founders who want to grow."
Right: "Solopreneurs who want their business to run without them."

Rule: if you can't name what they want in 7 words, you don't know
your hero.

---

### 2 · Has a problem (3 levels — name all three)

| Level | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **External** | The visible problem | "I can't get my Mondays back." |
| **Internal** | The feeling about the problem | "I feel trapped in my own business." |
| **Philosophical** | The injustice · why it shouldn't BE this way | "Founders shouldn't be slaves to the work they created." |

Most brands name only the external. The brands that win name all
three. Why? Because customers buy on the **internal** problem and
justify with the external. The philosophical layer makes them tell
their friends.

**Rule:** Always name a villain. Not a person — a force. "Endless
Mondays" or "Inbox tyranny" or "The DIY trap." The villain
externalizes the problem. The villain is what your offer defeats.

---

### 3 · Meets a guide (you · with empathy + authority)

Two ingredients. Skip either and you lose them.

**Empathy** · "I've been where you are."
- "I used to spend my Sundays planning my Mondays. I get it."

**Authority** · "And here's why I'm qualified to lead you out."
- "Over the past 3 years I've installed this system in 200+ businesses."

Rule: empathy first, authority second. Authority without empathy reads
as bragging. Empathy without authority reads as a peer.

---

### 4 · Who gives a plan (3-4 steps · simple)

The plan does ONE job: **reduce perceived risk** by showing the road.

Make it:
- 3 to 4 steps maximum
- Each step is an action verb
- Each step has a clear outcome
- The whole plan fits on one screen

Example (Purely Personal):
1. **Install** the plugin (15 min)
2. **Build your brain** (20 min)
3. **Run your first routine** (4 min)
4. **Watch your week run itself**

Rule: a 7-step plan reads as complicated. A 3-step plan reads as
inevitable.

---

### 5 · Calls them to action (direct + transitional)

**Direct CTA** · the buy. The decision moment.
- "Buy now" · "Book a call" · "Get started"

**Transitional CTA** · the safe step. For people not ready to buy.
- "Download the playbook" · "Watch the demo" · "Read the guide"

Use BOTH. On every page. Everywhere. Because at any moment 3% of
your audience is ready to buy and 97% needs more time. The direct CTA
catches the 3. The transitional CTA earns the 97.

Rule: the direct CTA must appear above the fold AND at the bottom of
every long page.

---

### 6 · Success (paint the after · sensory · specific)

What does life look like after they buy?

Bad: "You'll have more freedom."
Good: "Sunday at 5pm you close your laptop. Monday morning your AI
already wrote your posts, scored your leads, and prepped your
meetings. You drink your coffee and approve. The work is done before
you sat down."

Rule: use sensory detail. Sunday night. Coffee. Laptop closing.
Make them SEE the after.

---

### 7 · Failure (name what's at stake if they DON'T act)

Most marketers skip this. It's the most underrated lever in the
framework.

Pain of staying the same > pain of buying. That's the math of every
purchase.

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "If you don't buy, you'll miss out" | "If you don't change, you wake up next Sunday in the same panic. 12 months from now, your business will be the same size and you'll be more tired." |

Rule: name 3 specific failure outcomes. Not vague. Specific.

## Apply it · the BrandScript

For every business, fill in the 7 fields:

```
1 · CHARACTER:    Who is your hero (in one sentence)?
2 · PROBLEM:
   External:      ...
   Internal:      ...
   Philosophical: ...
3 · GUIDE:
   Empathy line:  "I've been ___"
   Authority line: "I've helped ___ achieve ___"
4 · PLAN:         3-4 steps, action verbs
5 · CTA:
   Direct:        ...
   Transitional:  ...
6 · SUCCESS:      paint the after, sensory
7 · FAILURE:      3 specific stakes if they don't act
```

This BrandScript is the source. Every page · every email · every post
draws from it.

---

## Apply it to /marketing-engine

When generating ANY long-form content (newsletter, sales page, video
script), structure as:

1. Hook = problem (external + internal · agitate the villain)
2. Section 1 = the guide (empathy + authority story)
3. Section 2 = the plan (3-4 steps)
4. Section 3 = success (sensory after-state)
5. Section 4 = failure (3 specific stakes)
6. CTA = direct + transitional

For LinkedIn posts (shorter), pick 3 of the 7 parts:
- Hook = problem (1+2)
- Body = guide + plan (3+4)
- CTA = direct OR transitional (5)

---

## Apply it to your homepage

Above the fold:
- Headline = What you offer + the dream outcome
- Sub-headline = The villain you defeat
- Direct CTA button = primary action
- Image = customer experiencing the after-state

Mid-page:
- "We've been there" empathy block
- "Here's the plan" 3-step graphic
- "Here's what changes" success section
- "Here's what stays the same if you don't" failure section
- Direct CTA + transitional CTA again

Bottom:
- Final direct CTA + risk reversal
