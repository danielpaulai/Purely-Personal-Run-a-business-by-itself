# Qualification Frameworks · BANT · MEDDIC · SPIN

> Three qualification frameworks. Each fits a different deal type.
> Use the wrong one and you'll burn 6 weeks on a deal that was never
> going to close.

---

## When to use which

| Framework | Best for | Deal size | Sales cycle |
|---|---|---|---|
| **BANT** | Transactional, single decision-maker | < $25k | < 30 days |
| **SPIN** | Mid-market, consultative sale | $25k - $100k | 30-90 days |
| **MEDDIC** | Enterprise, multi-stakeholder | > $100k | 90+ days |

For Purely Personal workshop ($49) + AI CEO ($1,999-2,999), **BANT is
the right choice** for the upsell. Below is each framework in detail
so you can apply them as your offers grow.

---

## BANT · Budget · Authority · Need · Timeline

The simplest. Created in the 1960s. Still works for transactional
deals because you only need 4 yes-answers to qualify.

### B · Budget

Can they afford it?

**Discovery questions:**
- "What's your budget for solving this?"
- "Have you allocated funds for this kind of solution?"
- "When you've solved problems like this before, what did it cost?"

**Red flags:**
- "We don't have a budget yet" → unqualified
- "Whatever it takes" → unqualified (no anchor)
- "We need it under $X" where X < your price → unqualified UNLESS you can negotiate

**Green flag:**
- They name a number that's within 50% of your price · you can close
  the gap

### A · Authority

Can THIS person say yes?

**Discovery questions:**
- "Who else is involved in this decision?"
- "Walk me through how decisions like this get made at your company."
- "If we agreed on the right solution today · what's the next step?"

**Red flags:**
- "I'd need to talk to my partner / boss / spouse" → not the decision-maker
- "We have a procurement process" → multi-step approval

**Green flag:**
- They use "I" pronouns when describing how they buy
- They've decided on similar purchases in the past

### N · Need

Do they have the problem you solve?

**Discovery questions:**
- "What's the situation that made you reach out?"
- "What happens if you don't solve this in 6 months?"
- "How is this affecting your team / revenue / time?"

**Red flags:**
- "Just exploring" → no real need yet
- "We're doing fine, just curious" → no pain
- Generic problem description → not specific enough to be real

**Green flag:**
- They name the problem in vivid, specific detail
- They've been suffering from it for 6+ months
- They've tried to solve it before

### T · Timeline

When do they need it solved?

**Discovery questions:**
- "What's your timeline for getting this in place?"
- "What changes if you don't have a solution by [date]?"
- "Is there an event / deadline / milestone driving this?"

**Red flags:**
- "Whenever" → no urgency
- "Maybe Q3 next year" → too far · they'll forget
- "ASAP" without a real reason → false urgency

**Green flag:**
- Real deadline (board meeting, product launch, end of quarter)
- "We need this in the next 30-60 days"

### BANT scoring

```
B · Budget       ___ / 25
A · Authority    ___ / 25
N · Need         ___ / 25
T · Timeline     ___ / 25
─────────────
Total            ___ / 100

90-100 · Hot · close immediately
70-89  · Warm · advance to demo / close
50-69  · Cool · nurture · re-qualify in 30 days
< 50   · Cold · do not pursue · move on
```

## SPIN Selling · Situation · Problem · Implication · Need-payoff

The mid-market evolution of BANT. Adds depth on the "why does this
matter" dimension.

### S · Situation Questions

Establish baseline. Understand current state.

- "How are you handling [problem area] now?"
- "What does your current setup look like?"
- "Walk me through your typical week."

**Trap:** asking too many. The buyer gets bored. 3-5 max.

### P · Problem Questions

Get them to NAME the friction.

- "What's NOT working with that approach?"
- "Where does the process tend to break down?"
- "What's the biggest challenge with how you do it now?"

### I · Implication Questions (the most important)

Surface the cost of NOT solving.

- "What happens if you don't fix this in the next 6 months?"
- "How is this affecting [revenue / morale / customer satisfaction]?"
- "Have you noticed this affecting other parts of the business?"
- "What's the dollar impact if this continues?"

This is where SPIN earns its money. Implication questions make the
cost of inaction visible. The buyer realizes the problem is bigger
than they thought.

### N · Need-payoff Questions

Get THEM to articulate the value of solving.

- "If we could solve [their problem] · what would that mean for [outcome]?"
- "How would your week look different if [pain] disappeared?"
- "What would it be worth to fix this in the next 30 days?"

The buyer sells THEMSELVES on the value. This is the sale before the
pitch.

## MEDDIC · the Enterprise Stack

For deals > $100k with multiple stakeholders. Each letter is a thing
you must verify before forecasting the deal.

### M · Metrics

What are the SPECIFIC, MEASURABLE outcomes the buyer expects?

- "If we delivered, what would success look like in numbers?"
- "What metrics would your boss measure this against?"

Without specific metrics, you can't prove value. You also can't avoid
"this didn't deliver" disputes after the close.

### E · Economic Buyer

Who actually has the money? Not who's CHAMPIONING the deal · who
SIGNS the check.

- "Who controls the budget for this initiative?"
- "Who has the final yes/no?"

Often this is NOT the person you're talking to. Get to them BEFORE
you spend 6 weeks on the deal.

### D · Decision Criteria

What are they comparing you against · and on what dimensions?

- "What's important in evaluating solutions like this?"
- "What does the ideal solution look like to you?"

You must know the criteria to position against them.

### D · Decision Process

What are the EXACT steps to close?

- "Walk me through the process from here to a signed contract."
- "Who reviews? Who approves? Who signs?"
- "How long does each step typically take?"

You can't forecast a close date without knowing this.

### I · Identify Pain

The same as SPIN's Implication. What's the cost of inaction?

### C · Champion

Who inside the company will FIGHT for your deal when you're not in
the room?

- "Who else has bought into this idea internally?"
- "Who would lose if we don't move forward?"

You need an internal advocate. Without one, the deal dies in the
final 10%.

## How to RUN a qualification call

Regardless of framework, the structure is the same:

```
1 · CONNECTION         · 3-5 min  · open the conversation
2 · QUALIFICATION      · 20-30 min · run the framework
3 · QUALIFY YOURSELF   · 5 min   · "are you a fit for me?"
4 · NEXT STEP          · 2 min   · clear ask for action
```

**The reverse qualification:** before you confirm the deal, ALSO
qualify yourself. "Based on what you've shared, I think I can help.
But let me ask · if I told you I couldn't help, what would your
backup plan be?"

This signals you're not desperate. Buyers respect sellers who can
walk away.

## Apply it to /sales-engine

When generating prospect research, the engine outputs a BANT score
(0-100) for each prospect. The score breakdown:

- B (25 pts) · derived from their company size, role, public funding
- A (25 pts) · derived from their title (VP+, founder, decision-maker)
- N (25 pts) · derived from job-board signals · recent posts about
  pain · current tool stack
- T (25 pts) · derived from triggering events (recent funding, new
  hire, product launch)

Top 3 prospects (BANT > 80) get 3-step outreach drafted.

For the AI CEO upsell during the workshop, treat workshop attendance
as a qualifying signal:
- B: $49 entry fee = pre-qualified for some budget
- A: solo founders = high authority
- N: came to "build a business that runs by itself" = active need
- T: workshop = active timeline (now)

So the workshop attendees are already BANT 75+. The pitch on Day 2
just needs to confirm the last 25 points (timeline + budget for the
$1,999 / $2,999 tier).
