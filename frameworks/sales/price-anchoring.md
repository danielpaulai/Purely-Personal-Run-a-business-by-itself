# Price Anchoring · The Stack of Value

> Price isn't a number. Price is what the buyer compares your offer
> against. Anchor to the right reference points · the price feels
> small. Anchor wrong · the same price feels insulting.

---

## The 3 anchoring moves

```
1 · ANCHOR HIGH    ·  reference an expensive comparable
2 · STACK VALUE    ·  build the offer to 5-10x the price
3 · DECOY EFFECT   ·  make your target tier the obvious choice
```

Use all 3 in the right order = a $1,999 offer feels like a steal.

---

## Move 1 · ANCHOR HIGH

The first number the prospect hears defines what comes next.

If they hear "$5,000" first · then $1,999 feels cheap.
If they hear "$49" first · then $1,999 feels expensive.

**The anchoring sequence:**

```
Step 1 · State the standalone retail value of each component
Step 2 · Sum to total value
Step 3 · Compare to common alternatives ("hiring an agency = $X")
Step 4 · THEN reveal your price
```

### Worked example for AI CEO ($1,999):

```
"Let me put a number on what's inside.

The AI CEO orchestration agent · alone, that's a $5,000 build if
you hired me to do it for you.

The 4-week sprint · live cohort calls · normally $1,500 in any
similar program.

The advanced engines pack · 10 engines × $200 each = $2,000.

Slack community · ongoing access · $97/mo industry standard ·
$1,164 over a year.

Group calls × 8 sessions × $300 = $2,400.

Even at the low end · you're looking at $12,000 of value.

Tier 1 is $1,999."
```

**The reveal of $1,999 AFTER $12,000** makes the same number feel
80%+ smaller than if you'd opened with it.

## Move 2 · STACK VALUE

The Grand Slam Offer (see grand-slam-offer.md) is a stack. Each
component:

1. Solves a specific problem
2. Has a standalone dollar value attached
3. Is positioned as a "bonus" (even if it's the core)
4. Stacks WITH other components, not instead of

**Rules of the stack:**

### Rule 1 · 3-7 components

- 1-2 components = the offer feels thin
- 3-7 = the sweet spot
- 8+ = the buyer stops believing the math

### Rule 2 · Each component has a name

Generic: "Plus you get coaching calls"
Anchored: "Plus the 4-Week Cohort Sprint with 8 group calls"

Names make components feel concrete and ownable.

### Rule 3 · Anchor each at retail price

Don't say: "This is included."
Say: "This is normally $1,500 if you bought it standalone."

Even if you've never sold it standalone · that's the comparable
market price. Use it.

### Rule 4 · Build to 5-10x the price

A good stack is 5x. A great stack is 10x. Stack value to:

- $10K stack → $1,999 price = 5x
- $20K stack → $1,999 price = 10x
- $30K+ stack → $1,999 price = 15x (starts to lose credibility)

10x is the sweet spot for credibility.

## Move 3 · THE DECOY EFFECT

When you offer 2 tiers, the smart move is to make Tier 1 a "decoy"
that makes Tier 2 the obvious choice.

### How decoys work (asymmetric dominance)

When prospects compare options, they pick the one that's CLEARLY
dominant on the dimensions they care about. Set the decoy correctly,
and Tier 2 becomes the obvious win.

**Example:**

```
TIER 1 · $1,999
- AI CEO orchestration
- 4-week sprint
- 10 advanced engines
- Group calls

TIER 2 · $2,999  ← target tier (you want this to be the most-bought)
- Everything in Tier 1
- 1:1 brain build with you (90 min)
- 1:1 AI CEO setup call (60 min)
- 90 days private 1:1 Slack with you
- First-3-routines done with you
- Priority on weekly calls
```

The math the buyer does:
- Tier 2 - Tier 1 = $1,000
- The added value (1:1 brain build + setup + 90-day Slack) = $5,000+ alone
- "I'd be stupid not to take Tier 2"

**Why this works:**
- Tier 2 makes Tier 1 look incomplete
- Tier 2's marginal cost ($1K) is dwarfed by its marginal value
- Buyers feel SMART picking Tier 2

**Result:** ~60-70% of upgraders go Tier 2 instead of Tier 1, even
though Tier 2 is 50% more expensive.

## The 3-tier decoy (advanced)

For higher-ticket offers, use 3 tiers:

```
TIER 1 · $499  ← decoy (looks expensive for what it is)
- Self-paced course
- No support
- No community

TIER 2 · $1,999 ← target tier #1 (most popular)
- Course + 4-week cohort + community + group calls

TIER 3 · $4,999 ← target tier #2 (premium · for the high-touch buyer)
- Tier 2 + 1:1 work + done-for-you setup
```

The Tier 1 makes Tier 2 look like a steal. The Tier 3 makes Tier 2
look reasonable. Result: 50% Tier 2, 30% Tier 3, 20% Tier 1.

## The contrast principle (Cialdini)

After a high anchor, ALL subsequent prices feel small. Use this:

**In conversation:**
- "We've worked with founders who pay $5K/month for ongoing
  consulting. This program is $1,999 one-time."

**In presentation:**
- Show a slide of "Hire an agency: $50K-$100K/year"
- Then your offer: $1,999 one-time
- The buyer's brain does the math automatically

**On the sales page:**
- "Most founders spend $20K+ trying to figure this out themselves
  in lost time and bad tools. This program is $1,999."

The contrast makes the price feel like a no-brainer.

## The price-justification line

After revealing the price · before the buyer can object · drop a
ONE-LINE justification:

```
"That's less than [common comparable] and gets you [primary outcome]
in [specific time]."
```

**Examples:**
- "$1,999 · less than 1 month of agency retainer · for a system
  that runs your business for the next 12 months."
- "$1,999 · less than the cost of one bad hire · for the OS
  underneath your next 5 years."

This pre-empts the price objection by reframing the comparison.

## What NOT to do (anchoring mistakes)

### 1 · Lead with price

> "Hey, our program is $1,999 · here's what's inside."

Wrong. The buyer's brain anchors to $1,999 immediately. Everything
that comes after has to JUSTIFY it. Reverse this · always reveal
price LAST.

### 2 · Discount mid-pitch

> "It's normally $2,499 but I'll do $1,999 today only."

Wrong. Discounts feel arbitrary · the buyer wonders what the "real"
price is. Better: state $1,999 firmly, with the stack at $12K
value. No discount needed.

### 3 · Compare yourself to free alternatives

> "Most courses out there are free, but ours is $1,999 because..."

Wrong. You just anchored to $0. Now you have to justify $1,999
against $0 · which is impossible. Always anchor against PAID
comparables.

### 4 · Make the stack feel padded

> "Plus you get a free PDF guide ($297 value)..."

Wrong. PDF guides aren't worth $297. The buyer's BS detector flags
it. Every stack item must feel like it could legitimately be sold
standalone.

### 5 · Fake "won't last" pricing

> "$1,999 today only · tomorrow it goes back to $4,999!"

Wrong. The buyer has seen this on every funnel · they don't
believe it. Use REAL urgency (cohort start date, doors close at X
real time, next cohort 3 months away) instead.

## The anchor-stack-decoy script for the workshop close

```
1 · "Let me put a number on the value." (anchor setup)

2 · Walk through stack: $5K + $1.5K + $2K + $1.16K + $2.4K = $12K
   (anchor high)

3 · "Tier 1 is $1,999. Tier 2 is $2,999."
   (price reveal · price-anchor → contrast)

4 · "The reason I price it like this: this offer is for the 30
   founders in this room. After 13:00 · both close. Next cohort,
   if I run one, $2,999 base. So $1,999 is only happening today."
   (real urgency)

5 · "If you do the work for 30 days and your business doesn't
   shift · refund. No defending. The risk is on me · not you."
   (risk reversal · de-anchors price)

6 · "If you can see how this works for YOU · stand up."
   (decisive close)
```

That sequence converts ~12-15% of a warm room. Mess up step 1 (lead
with price) or step 5 (no risk reversal) · drops to 3-5%.

## Apply it to /sales-engine

When the engine is preparing offer presentation, it should:

1. **Audit the offer** against the stack rules
2. **Generate the stack** with retail anchors per component
3. **Sum to $X total value**
4. **Generate 2-3 tier structure** with decoy logic
5. **Write the price-justification line** specific to the offer
6. **Output the close script** with anchor + stack + decoy + close
7. **Output the urgency framing** based on real cohort timing

The engine should NEVER output a price without the surrounding
anchor structure. Bare prices are how deals die.
