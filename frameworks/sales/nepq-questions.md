# NEPQ · Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questions

> The single most-underrated sales method of the past decade. Built
> around questions, not pitches. The seller speaks ~30% of the call.
> The buyer talks themselves into buying.

---

## The premise

Traditional sales: TELL. Modern sales: ASK.
- Old: "Here's why our product is great."
- New: "Tell me what's NOT working with your current setup."

The buyer trusts what THEY say more than what YOU say. NEPQ engineers
the call so the buyer says everything you'd want to say.

---

## The 3-stage call structure

```
STAGE 1 · CONNECTION       ·  3-5 min  ·  open the conversation
STAGE 2 · ENGAGEMENT       ·  20-30 min ·  uncover the pain · cost · urgency
STAGE 3 · TRANSITION       ·  5-10 min ·  commit + present + close
```

NEPQ shifts ~70% of the call into Stage 2. That's where the buyer
sells themselves.

---

## Stage 1 · CONNECTION (3-5 min · open the conversation)

Goal: lower their guard. Establish you're NOT a pushy salesperson.

**The Connection Opener:**

> "Hey [name], appreciate you booking the call. Quick question
> before we get into anything · what made you reach out?"

That's it. ONE question. The buyer talks first. Their answer
determines the entire rest of the call.

**Why this works:**
- Most reps open with a feature pitch. NEPQ opens with curiosity.
- The buyer's first answer reveals their pain in their language.
- You haven't pitched anything yet · so they don't have a wall up.

**Common buyer answers:**
- "I saw your post about [X] and thought I'd see if you could help."
- "We're looking at solutions to [problem]."
- "Honestly · just exploring."

Whatever they say, your follow-up is always the same: **"Tell me
more about that."**

---

## Stage 2 · ENGAGEMENT (the heart of NEPQ · 20-30 min)

5 question types. In this order. You can loop back, but you must hit
all 5.

### Question Type 1 · SITUATION QUESTIONS

What does their current setup look like? Open · neutral · no judgment.

**Examples:**
- "How are you handling [problem area] right now?"
- "Walk me through your current process."
- "How long have you been doing it that way?"
- "Who else is involved in that?"

**Goal:** establish the baseline. You can't propose change until you
understand the current state.

**Trap to avoid:** asking too many situation questions. The buyer gets
bored. 3-5 max, then move to Problem.

---

### Question Type 2 · PROBLEM QUESTIONS

What's NOT working in the current setup? Where is the friction?

**Examples:**
- "What's the biggest challenge with that approach?"
- "What's NOT working about the way you're doing it now?"
- "If there was ONE thing you could fix in your current setup, what would it be?"
- "Where do things tend to break down?"

**Goal:** the buyer NAMES the problem in their own words. Now it's
their problem · not your pitch.

**Power move:** when they name a problem, ask **"How long has that
been an issue?"** The longer the problem has existed, the higher the
emotional weight.

---

### Question Type 3 · CONSEQUENCE QUESTIONS (the most important type)

What happens IF the problem doesn't get solved? What does it cost?

**Examples:**
- "What happens if you don't solve this in the next 6 months?"
- "What's the cost of leaving things the way they are?"
- "How is this affecting [revenue / team / family / sleep]?"
- "Have you seen this affect [downstream outcome]?"

**Goal:** make the cost of inaction visible and visceral. The buyer
realizes the problem is bigger than they were treating it.

**Why this matters:** people don't buy to gain · they buy to avoid
loss. Consequence questions surface the loss they're already
incurring.

**Sample sequence:**
> Buyer: "Our follow-up process is bad."
> You: "What's that costing you?"
> Buyer: "We probably leave 30% of deals on the table."
> You: "What's 30% of your deal volume mean in dollars?"
> Buyer: "Maybe... $200k a year."
> You: "How long has that been the case?"
> Buyer: "Honestly · about 18 months."
> You: "So roughly $300k of revenue you've left on the table?"
> (silence · the buyer feels it land)

That moment is the inflection point of the entire call.

---

### Question Type 4 · QUALIFYING QUESTIONS

Are they actually a fit? Can they pull the trigger?

**Examples:**
- "Have you tried to fix this before?"
- "What stopped you?"
- "What would it look like if this was solved?"
- "Who else needs to be part of this decision?"
- "What's your timeline for getting this addressed?"
- "What does your budget look like for solving this?"

**Goal:** confirm they're actually a real opportunity. Filter out
tire-kickers without saying "are you a tire-kicker?"

**Trap:** asking these too early. If you ask about budget before you've
established the cost of NOT solving (Q3), the buyer perceives the
budget as too high. After Q3, the budget feels small relative to the
cost of inaction.

---

### Question Type 5 · TRANSITION QUESTIONS

Bridge to the close.

**Examples:**
- "If we could solve [their named problem] · would you be open to seeing how?"
- "If I showed you a way to [their desired outcome] · would that be worth a closer look?"
- "Is this something you'd want to fix in the next [their named timeline]?"

**Goal:** get a verbal yes BEFORE you present anything. Now the
presentation is in response to THEIR ask · not your pitch.

When they say yes · move to Stage 3.

---

## Stage 3 · TRANSITION (5-10 min · present + close)

Now · and only now · do you talk about your offer.

**Structure:**
1. **Reframe their problem** in your words: "Based on what you said,
   the situation is [paraphrase]. The cost is [paraphrase]. Is that
   right?"
2. **Bridge to your solution**: "Here's what we do for situations
   like yours."
3. **Present** in 3-4 bullet points · NOT a feature dump
4. **Pause** · "What questions does that raise?"
5. **Handle objections** (see `objection-handling.md`)
6. **Decisive close**: "Based on everything we talked about · does
   this feel like a fit?"

**The decisive close:** ASK FOR THE YES. Don't end with "what do you
want to do?" End with "are you in or are you out?" The buyer will
respect the directness if you've earned it through the previous 25
minutes.

---

## The 70/30 rule

Track your talk-to-listen ratio:
- Reps who close < 30%: talk 70%+ of the time
- Reps who close > 50%: talk < 30% of the time

Record your calls. Time how long YOU talk. If you're over 30%, you're
selling on features. NEPQ flips the ratio.

---

## NEPQ-style cold outreach (compressed)

For DMs / cold emails, compress NEPQ to 3 sentences:

```
Sentence 1 · Specific observation about their situation (Situation Q)
Sentence 2 · Reflect what's likely not working (Problem Q)
Sentence 3 · Soft ask for a conversation (Transition Q)
```

Example:
> "Hey [name], saw you just hit $1M ARR · congrats. Most founders at
> that point start drowning in operations · is that what's happening
> for you? If so, open to a 15-min walk-through of how I help
> founders cross the $2M line without burning out?"

When the engine preps a sales call (Thursday Meeting Prep), it should
output:

1. **3 Situation questions** tailored to the prospect (from their
   LinkedIn scrape)
2. **3 Problem questions** specific to their likely friction
3. **2 Consequence questions** with framing for their dollar amounts
4. **2 Qualifying questions** customized to deal type
5. **1 Transition question** with the prospect's name dropped in

For prospect research output, the engine should generate the
NEPQ-style outreach (3 sentences) as the FIRST option.

---

## When NOT to use NEPQ

NEPQ is wrong for:
- Transactional purchases under $100 (too long)
- Self-serve products with high product-led growth (waste of effort)
- Audiences in active "ready to buy now" mode (just close them)
- Dealing with technical buyers who hate fluff (compress hard)

---

## The talk-track to memorize

You should be able to run a NEPQ call without notes. Memorize the
question stems:

1. "What made you reach out?"
2. "Tell me more about that."
3. "How are you handling [X] right now?"
4. "What's the biggest challenge with that?"
5. "What happens if you don't solve this in 6 months?"
6. "How long has that been an issue?"
7. "Have you tried to fix this before?"
8. "If we could solve [X], would you want to see how?"

Eight stems. Run a complete sales call. Master them and the rest is
follow-up.
