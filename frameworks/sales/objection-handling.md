# Objection Handling · The 5 Universal Objections

> Every objection is one of 5. Memorize the 5. Pre-script the
> responses. Handle them BEFORE they come up · or in real-time when
> they do.

---

## The 5 universal objections

```
1 · TIME      · "I need to think about it"
2 · MONEY     · "I can't afford it / it's too expensive"
3 · NEED      · "I'm not sure if I really need this"
4 · TRUST     · "I'm not sure this will work for me"
5 · AUTHORITY · "I need to talk to my [partner / boss]"
```

Every objection collapses into one of these. When the prospect says
ANYTHING that sounds like resistance, your job is to identify which
of the 5 it is, then run the script.

---

## Objection 1 · TIME · "I need to think about it"

Translation: "I'm not convinced yet. Buy me an out."

**The wrong move:** "Take all the time you need."
That's polite. It also kills the deal. 80% of "I need to think about
it" buyers never come back.

**The right move:** acknowledge → specify → re-engage.

```
SCRIPT:

Buyer: "I need to think about it."

You: "Totally fair. While you're thinking · what's the SPECIFIC
question you'd need answered to decide one way or the other?
Type it in chat. I'll answer it · then you can think about it."
```

**Why this works:**
- Most "I need to think about it" objections are vague stalls.
- The specific question forces them to articulate the real concern.
- Once articulated, you can address it.
- If they CAN'T name a specific question · they're not actually
  thinking. They're avoiding.

**Variant for live workshop close:**

> "Beautiful. While you think · what's the specific question that
> would tip you over? Drop it in chat. I'll answer all of them in
> the next 10 minutes."

---

## Objection 2 · MONEY · "I can't afford it"

Translation: "The cost feels bigger than the perceived value."

There are two flavors of money objection:

### a · "I literally can't afford it" (cash-tight)

These are not your customers. Move on. Don't try to discount. Don't
try to payment-plan unless that's already a built-in option. Tell
them:

> "I appreciate you being upfront. This isn't the right moment for
> you · totally fair. When the timing's right, I'll be here."

Note their name. Re-engage in 90 days.

### b · "It feels expensive" (perception-tight)

These ARE your customers. They're saying the price feels high
relative to perceived value. Your job: increase perceived value, not
lower price.

**SCRIPT:**

> Buyer: "It's a lot of money."
>
> You: "I hear you. Let me ask · what does staying where you are
> cost over the next 12 months? In time you don't get back, in
> revenue you don't ship, in Sundays you spend planning Mondays?
> If that number is bigger than $1,999 · the question isn't whether
> you can afford it. It's whether you can afford NOT to."

**Why this works:**
- Reframes price vs cost-of-inaction
- Forces them to do the math themselves
- The math almost always favors buying

**Variant · the contrast:**

> "You spent 4 hours of your weekend in this workshop. By Monday at
> 9am, you'll have run your first marketing engine. That alone has
> already paid you back $49 in time saved. AI CEO compounds that
> for the next 12 months. The question isn't price · it's leverage."

## Objection 3 · NEED · "I'm not sure I really need this"

Translation: "I don't yet feel the cost of NOT having it."

This is a NEPQ Stage-2 problem · the Implication / Consequence
questions weren't strong enough. Re-run them.

**SCRIPT:**

> Buyer: "I'm not sure if I really need this right now."
>
> You: "Fair. Let me ask · what happens to your business in 6 months
> if you don't have a system that runs without you? Where will you
> be on Monday morning, Sept 1, if you've still got 80-hour weeks?
> Same revenue? Same energy?
>
> If the answer is 'fine, exactly the same' · then you don't need
> this. I'd say so. But if the answer is 'tired and behind' · the
> need is real. The question is when, not if."

**Why this works:**
- Forces them into the future · which makes the need vivid
- Honest framing ("if you don't need it I'd say so")
- The buyer answers the question themselves
- Their own answer is more persuasive than yours

## Objection 4 · TRUST · "I'm not sure this will work for ME"

Translation: "I see it works for others. I don't believe it'll work
for me specifically."

This is the Perfect Webinar's Question 1 (vehicle) and Question 2
(am I capable) · they weren't convinced.

**SCRIPT:**

> Buyer: "I love it but I'm not sure it works for [my niche / my
> stage / my situation]."
>
> You: "Tell me about your situation specifically. What's making you
> think it won't work?"

LISTEN. They'll tell you exactly what to address. Then:

> "Got it. Here's an example · [find a case study with similar
> profile]. They had the same concern. Here's what happened in their
> first 30 days: [specifics]. Same mechanism. Same engines. Same
> cohort. Different niche, same outcome."

If you genuinely don't have a case study for their specific
situation · be honest:

> "Honestly, I haven't worked with someone in your exact niche yet.
> But the mechanism is universal · it's about installing engines
> that read your business and act on it. The niche shapes the
> output, not the system. Want to test the assumption with a 30-day
> pilot · with the full money-back guarantee?"

The honesty + the risk reversal closes the deal.

## Objection 5 · AUTHORITY · "I need to talk to my [partner / boss]"

Translation: "I'm not the decision-maker, OR I'm using this as a
stall."

This is fundamentally a BANT-A miss · you should have qualified
authority earlier. Now you handle it in real-time.

**Diagnosis question:**

> "Of course · who else is part of the decision?"

Their answer reveals which version it is:

### a · Real partner / boss (legitimate stakeholder)

> Buyer: "I need to talk to my husband."
>
> You: "Got it. What's HIS biggest question likely to be?"

Listen. Then:

> "Beautiful. Two options · one, text him right now · the link is in
> chat · he can see it himself. Two, DM me his question · I'll
> record a 60-second video answering it before doors close."

This does two things:
- Reduces friction (the partner can see the offer directly)
- Makes the seller's video the answer to the partner's objection
- Prevents the deal dying in a "I forgot to talk to him" loop

### b · Stall disguised as authority

> Buyer: "I need to think about it AND check with my partner."

That's a TIME objection wearing AUTHORITY clothing. Run the TIME
script (Objection 1).

## Pre-handling objections (the 80% rule)

Don't WAIT for objections. PRE-handle them. 80% of objections that
get raised in real-time can be neutralized in your pitch script.

**Where to pre-handle:**

| Objection | Pre-handle moment in pitch |
|---|---|
| TIME | "This decision doesn't need to be hard. By the end of this hour, you'll have everything you need to know. Yes or no in 30 minutes." |
| MONEY | "I'm going to walk you through what's inside · then the price. The price will feel small compared to the value. That's intentional." |
| NEED | The PROVOKE block · agitate the cost of inaction (see SALES-SEQUENCE.md) |
| TRUST | "Here's what 200 founders who installed this look like 90 days later · [case studies × 3]" |
| AUTHORITY | "If you need to bring someone into this · I'll send you the link to share. Both of you can be on the next call." |

By the time you finish the pitch, all 5 should be addressed.

## The objection-handling muscle

The fastest way to get good at objection handling: **record your sales
calls, transcribe them, find the objections, write the perfect
response, drill it.**

Top sellers practice objection responses like comedians practice
material. You should be able to deliver each of the 5 responses in
your sleep.

**Drill exercise:**
- Print the 5 objection scripts
- Have a friend read the objections at random
- Respond OUT LOUD (not in your head)
- Record yourself
- Play back · refine · repeat

90 minutes of drilling = 6 months of unintentional practice.
