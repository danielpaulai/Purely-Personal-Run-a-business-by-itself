# Pricing Frameworks · 5 Methods to Set Prices That Work

> Most operators price by feel. They underprice by 30-50%. They miss
> the math entirely. Here are 5 frameworks that turn pricing into a
> system · not a guess.

---

## The 5 frameworks

```
1 · COST-PLUS        ·  cost × markup (only for commodity)
2 · COMPETITIVE      ·  compare to market alternatives
3 · VALUE-BASED      ·  % of value delivered (the master)
4 · ANCHORING        ·  high anchor reframes everything else
5 · DECOY EFFECT     ·  asymmetric dominance · 3-tier strategy
```

For the AI CEO upsell · use #3 (value-based) + #4 (anchoring) +
#5 (decoy). #1 and #2 are weaker for high-value digital products.

---

## 1 · COST-PLUS PRICING

The simplest. Price = cost × (1 + markup %).

**Example:**
- Cost to deliver: $200
- Markup target: 5x
- Price: $1,000

**When to use:**
- Commodity products
- Pass-through services with clear costs
- Manufacturing

**Why it's WEAK for digital products:**
- Marginal cost of digital is near-zero
- 5x of zero is still zero
- Doesn't capture the VALUE you create

For AI CEO · cost-plus would price the program at $300
(cost-to-deliver × 5) · which is 85% under-priced. Don't use this
framework for digital / coaching.

---

## 2 · COMPETITIVE PRICING

Look at competitors. Price ±10% of them.

**When it works:**
- Mature markets with similar offerings
- Customer comparison-shopping is high
- You're not differentiated enough to charge premium

**When it fails:**
- Your offer is uniquely positioned
- You compete on transformation, not features
- Competitors are themselves under-priced (most are)

**For Purely Personal:**
- Generic AI workshops: $97-$497
- Cohort programs: $1,500-$5,000
- Done-with-you AI consulting: $5,000-$20,000

You're a hybrid. Workshop ($49) is below most. AI CEO ($1,999/$2,999)
is in the cohort program range. You can charge premium because the
deliverable is more specific.

**Caution:** competitive pricing is RECEIVED wisdom · it doesn't
capture VALUE. Use as a sanity check · not the primary method.

---

## 3 · VALUE-BASED PRICING (the master framework)

Price = some % of the value your customer captures from your offer.

```
VALUE-BASED PRICE = customer's expected ROI × capture %
```

**The capture % rule:** charge 10-30% of the value you deliver.
Below 10% · you're under-priced (leaving money on the table).
Above 30% · you're vulnerable to competitors.

**Worked example for AI CEO:**

The customer's expected ROI from AI CEO over 12 months:
- Time saved: 20 hrs/wk × 50 wks × $50/hr = $50,000
- Revenue added (better systems → more capacity): $20,000
- Cost saved (less burnout, fewer hires): $10,000
                                          ───────
Total expected ROI:                         $80,000

If you capture 5%: price = $4,000
If you capture 3%: price = $2,400
If you capture 2%: price = $1,600

**Conclusion:** AI CEO at $1,999-$2,999 is well-anchored to value-based
pricing · you're capturing ~3% of the $80K-100K value.

**For higher-ticket / smaller markets:** you can capture 10-30%. A
$5K offer that produces $50K of value is a no-brainer.

---

## How to FIND your value number (the discovery questions)

In your sales call (or pre-purchase survey), ask:

```
1 · "What does solving this problem mean for your business in dollars?"
2 · "What's it currently costing you (in time, lost revenue, mistakes)?"
3 · "If we 10x'd your [outcome], what's that worth over the next year?"
4 · "What's the cost of NOT solving this?"
```

Capture their NUMBERS. Use them in your pitch ("you said X · we charge
3% of X · here's the math").

---

## 4 · ANCHORING PRICING (the contrast trick)

Price perception is relative. Whatever number they hear FIRST defines
what comes after.

**The anchoring sequence:**

1 · State a high reference point ("Most agencies charge $50K/year")
2 · State the value of your offer ("This delivers $80K of value")
3 · Reveal your price ("$1,999 · one time")

The buyer's brain auto-compares · $1,999 vs $50K vs $80K. Each
comparison makes your price feel smaller.

**Anchor sources:**
- Industry alternatives ("hiring an agency: $X")
- Time-cost equivalent ("20 hours of consulting at $200/hr = $4K")
- Lost-opportunity cost ("not solving this for 12 months costs you $80K")
- Premium tier of your own offer ("Tier 2 is $4,999 · this is Tier 1")

**Rule:** never reveal price BEFORE the anchor. Bare prices feel
expensive. Anchored prices feel like deals.

See `frameworks/sales/price-anchoring.md` for the full anchoring
playbook.

## 5 · THE DECOY EFFECT (3-tier strategy)

Use 3 pricing tiers · structured so the middle tier is the obvious
winner.

**The structure:**

```
TIER 1 ·  small  ·  meant to look incomplete
TIER 2 ·  TARGET ·  designed to be the most-bought
TIER 3 ·  large  ·  meant to make Tier 2 look reasonable
```

**Worked example:**

```
TIER 1 · WORKSHOP ($49)
- 2-day live workshop
- Plugin install
- 5 engines + 6 routines
- (no AI CEO · no cohort · no 1:1)

TIER 2 · AI CEO COHORT ($1,999)  ← TARGET (most-bought)
- Everything in workshop
- AI CEO orchestration setup
- 4-week cohort + group calls
- Slack community
- 30-day money-back

TIER 3 · AI CEO + 1:1 ($2,999)
- Everything in Tier 2
- 1:1 brain-build call (90 min)
- 1:1 AI CEO setup (60 min)
- 90 days private 1:1 Slack
- First-3-routines done with you
```

**The math the buyer does:**
- Tier 1 to 2: $1,950 jump for huge value (cohort, AI CEO, community)
- Tier 2 to 3: $1,000 jump for 1:1 attention (worth more than $1K)
- Tier 1 looks incomplete, Tier 3 looks premium, Tier 2 looks JUST RIGHT

**Result:** ~50% pick Tier 2, ~30% pick Tier 3, ~20% pick Tier 1.

The Tier 1 + Tier 3 are decoys that make Tier 2 (your target) feel
like the smart choice.

## The price-elasticity test

How sensitive is your customer to price?

**The Van Westendorp 4-question survey** (run with 30+ existing
customers):

```
1 · At what price would this offer be SO CHEAP you'd question quality?
2 · At what price is it a STEAL · clearly cheap but you'd buy?
3 · At what price is it EXPENSIVE but you'd still buy because of value?
4 · At what price is it SO EXPENSIVE you wouldn't buy?
```

Plot the responses on a graph. Where the "expensive but buy" line
crosses the "steal" line · that's your sweet spot. Where "expensive"
crosses "too expensive" · that's your max.

For digital coaching products in 2026 · the sweet spot is typically
$1,500-$3,000. Below = perceived as low quality. Above = budget
resistance for the audience size.

---

## When to RAISE prices

You're under-priced if any of these are true:
- LTV/CAC > 4 (you have margin to capture)
- Close rate > 50% (no friction · price too low)
- Customers regularly say "you should charge more"
- Customers don't ask about price during the sale
- You're working harder than your peers at similar revenue

The default move every 6 months: **raise prices 20-30%**. Most
businesses can sustain this without losing customers · because price
isn't the primary buying driver for value-based offerings.

---

## When to LOWER prices (rare · be careful)

Lower prices ONLY when:
- LTV/CAC < 2 (you're losing on every customer)
- Close rate < 10% (you're way over the value bar)
- Market is contracting (entering downturn · cash preservation)

Almost never lower as a STRATEGY. Lower = chase the wrong customer.
Better: keep prices · improve targeting + offer.

---

## The pricing audit (run every 6 months)

```
[ ] What's our LTV/CAC?
[ ] What's our close rate?
[ ] What did customers say in the last 30 days about price?
[ ] What % of value are we capturing?
[ ] Where do we sit vs competitors?
[ ] When did we last raise prices?
[ ] What's the elasticity test telling us?
```

Use the audit to set the next 6 months' prices. Don't set and
forget. Pricing is a system, not an event.

## The 1-line pricing rule

> "Price is what they pay. Value is what they get. The gap is your
> moat."

Your price should feel **expensive but obvious** · meaning the customer
knows it's a real investment AND knows it's worth it. If they hesitate
on price · the value isn't communicated. If they don't blink at price ·
you're under-priced.

The sweet spot: 1-2 second pause, then "yes."
