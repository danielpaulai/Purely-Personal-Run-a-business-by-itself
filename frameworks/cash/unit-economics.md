# Unit Economics · The 5 Numbers Every Founder Must Know

> Most operators run their business on intuition. Vibes. "It feels
> like a good month." That's fine until you scale. Then vibes
> bankrupt you. The 5 unit-economics numbers tell you the truth.

---

## The 5 numbers

```
1 · CAC          ·  Customer Acquisition Cost
2 · LTV          ·  Lifetime Value (per customer)
3 · LTV/CAC      ·  the ratio · the master metric
4 · GROSS MARGIN ·  $ from each sale that's actually profit
5 · PAYBACK      ·  months until CAC is recovered
```

Calculate these once a month. If you don't know them · you're
guessing.

---

## 1 · CAC · Customer Acquisition Cost

How much do you spend (in money + time) to get ONE customer?

```
CAC = (total marketing $ + sales $) / new customers acquired
```

**Worked example for Purely Personal:**

```
THIS QUARTER:
Marketing spend (ads, tools, contractors):   $1,500
Sales effort (your time × $50/hr × 60 hrs):  $3,000
                                             ──────
Total acquisition cost:                      $4,500

New paying customers acquired:               30 (workshop)
                                             ────
CAC for $49 workshop:                        $150 per customer

(But the workshop is the funnel · not the product)
```

For the AI CEO upsell:

```
Same quarter spend:                          $4,500
AI CEO conversions (from workshop):          5
                                             ────
CAC for AI CEO:                              $900 per customer

(Lower than it looks · the workshop revenue offsets some CAC)
```

**Adjusted CAC** (after netting workshop revenue):

```
Total spend:                                 $4,500
Less workshop revenue:                       $1,470
                                             ──────
Net spend:                                   $3,030
Divided by AI CEO customers:                 5
                                             ────
True CAC for AI CEO:                         $606
```

**Rule:** always include YOUR time at a market rate. CAC ignoring
founder time is fantasy.

## 2 · LTV · Lifetime Value

How much does ONE customer pay you over the time they're a customer?

```
LTV = average revenue per customer × average lifetime (months)
```

For one-time products:
```
LTV = price (one transaction)
```

For subscription/cohort products with repeat purchases:
```
LTV = (avg monthly rev per customer) × (avg # months as customer)
    + (avg upsell value × upsell rate)
```

**Worked example · AI CEO:**

```
Initial purchase:                $1,999 - $2,999 (avg $2,300)
Average upsell to next cohort:   $0 - $1,500 (avg $300 weighted)
Lifetime · cohort + alumni:      ~12 months
                                 ────
Total LTV:                       ~$2,600 per AI CEO customer
```

If you add a continuity component (monthly Slack access at $97):

```
LTV = $2,300 (initial) + $97 × 12 months × 60% retain = $2,998
```

**Rule:** be conservative. Use realistic retention numbers · not
hopeful ones.

---

## 3 · LTV/CAC · the master metric

```
LTV/CAC = Lifetime Value / Customer Acquisition Cost
```

**Interpretation:**
- LTV/CAC < 1 · you LOSE money on every customer · death
- LTV/CAC = 1 · break-even · also death (no margin for ops)
- LTV/CAC = 2 · marginal · need to improve
- LTV/CAC = 3 · GOOD · most healthy SaaS / coaching businesses
- LTV/CAC = 4-5 · GREAT · efficient acquisition
- LTV/CAC > 5 · either you're under-investing in growth · or you've
  found a goldmine

**Purely Personal AI CEO math:**

```
LTV:       $2,998
CAC:       $606
─────────────────
Ratio:     4.95

Interpretation: GREAT · room to spend more on acquisition
```

If LTV/CAC was 4.95, you could spend up to ~$1,500 per customer
(getting ratio to 2:1 minimum) and still have a healthy business.
That tells you you can afford ads, more aggressive sales effort, or
better referral incentives.

## 4 · GROSS MARGIN

```
Gross margin = (revenue - cost of goods sold) / revenue
```

What % of each dollar of revenue is actually yours after the direct
costs of delivering?

**For digital / service businesses:**

```
$1,999 program revenue
- Stripe fees (2.9% + $0.30):    $58
- Software cost (per customer):  $20
- Bonus content licensing:       $10
- Cohort delivery contractor:    $50
                                 ──────
Direct costs:                    $138

Gross margin:                    ($1,999 - $138) / $1,999 = 93%
```

**Industry benchmarks:**
- SaaS: 70-90%
- Coaching/Service: 60-90% (depending on delivery model)
- Physical products: 30-60%
- Marketplaces: 10-30%

For Purely Personal · 90%+ is realistic for AI CEO. The cohort
delivery is YOU + AI · low marginal cost.

## 5 · PAYBACK PERIOD

How many months until you've earned back the CAC?

```
Payback = CAC / monthly contribution per customer
```

For one-time products · payback is "immediately" (or "first sale").

For subscription / cohort products:

```
CAC:                  $606
Monthly margin per customer:   $200 (Slack subscription × 90% margin)
                              ─────
Payback:                       3 months
```

**Benchmarks:**
- < 12 months · GREAT · efficient growth
- 12-18 months · acceptable for SaaS
- 18-24 months · funded / strategic plays
- > 24 months · probably too long · re-think the unit economics

**Why payback matters:** it tells you how fast cash recycles. If
payback is 3 months · you can fund the next $606 of CAC from the
revenue of customers acquired 3 months ago. Compounding.

If payback is 24 months · you need 24 months of capital before each
acquisition pays back. Cash-intensive.

## How to FIX bad unit economics

### If LTV is too low:
- Raise prices (most operators are 30-50% under-priced)
- Add upsells / continuity (Slack subscription · advanced cohorts)
- Improve retention (fix product · reduce churn)
- Cross-sell to alumni

### If CAC is too high:
- Improve targeting (less spray, more spear)
- Use referrals (warm CAC < cold CAC)
- Lower funnel friction (shorter sales cycle)
- Build organic flywheel (content compounding)

### If gross margin is too low:
- Renegotiate vendor contracts
- Eliminate low-margin offerings
- Automate delivery (engines vs humans)
- Raise prices

### If payback is too long:
- Charge upfront (annual vs monthly)
- Add a high-margin entry product (workshop → AI CEO)
- Shorten sales cycle
- Reduce CAC

## The 4 health zones

A business is in one of 4 zones based on its unit economics:

```
ZONE 1 · UNHEALTHY     · LTV/CAC < 2 · payback > 24 months
ZONE 2 · STABLE        · LTV/CAC 2-3 · payback 12-24 months
ZONE 3 · HEALTHY       · LTV/CAC 3-4 · payback < 12 months
ZONE 4 · COMPOUND      · LTV/CAC > 4 · payback < 6 months · margin > 80%
```

Most VC-backed SaaS targets Zone 3. Most great solopreneur
businesses (with engines + automation) live in Zone 4. The Purely
Personal AI CEO program is engineered for Zone 4.
