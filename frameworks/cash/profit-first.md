# Profit First · The 5-Account Cash System

> Most businesses are unprofitable not because of revenue, but
> because of cash flow design. Profit First flips the math: pay
> profit FIRST, then operate on what's left. 5 bank accounts. Same
> formula. Forever.

---

## The wrong formula (most businesses)

```
Revenue - Expenses = Profit
```

Sounds right. It's wrong. Because:
- "Profit" is whatever's left at the end (often nothing)
- Expenses expand to consume available cash
- Profit becomes a hope, not a system

Result: profitable on paper, broke in the bank.

---

## The Profit First formula

```
Revenue - Profit = Expenses
```

Take profit FIRST. Live on what's left. Force discipline by design.

If you only have 30% left for operations · you'll find a way to run
on 30%. The cap creates the discipline. The discipline creates the
profit.

---

## The 5 bank accounts

```
1 · INCOME       ·  every dollar lands here first
2 · PROFIT       ·  taken out first · transferred to a separate
                    untouchable account
3 · OWNER PAY    ·  YOUR salary · taken out next · transferred
4 · TAX          ·  taxes · transferred to its own account · 
                    untouched until tax day
5 · OPEX         ·  what's left · pays for everything else
```

5 separate bank accounts. Real accounts. Set up at your bank. Money
moves between them on a schedule. The friction IS the system.

---

## The percentages (target allocations)

For a small business:

| Account | Real-Revenue % |
|---|---|
| **Profit** | 5% |
| **Owner Pay** | 50% |
| **Tax** | 15% |
| **OpEx** | 30% |

These are starting targets. Higher revenue · profit % goes up.
Service businesses · OpEx % goes down. Adjust over 12-24 months.

**Real-Revenue** = revenue minus pass-through costs (e.g., agencies
have media buy as pass-through · subtract before applying %s).

For solopreneurs (no team, low overhead):

| Account | % |
|---|---|
| Profit | 10% |
| Owner Pay | 60% |
| Tax | 15% |
| OpEx | 15% |

For agency / team-heavy:

| Account | % |
|---|---|
| Profit | 5% |
| Owner Pay | 25% |
| Tax | 15% |
| OpEx | 55% |

## The schedule · 10/25 transfers

Twice a month, on the 10th and 25th, you transfer money from
INCOME to the other 4 accounts according to the percentages.

**Example:**

INCOME account balance on the 10th: $20,000

Transfers:
```
PROFIT (10%):     $2,000  →  PROFIT account
OWNER PAY (60%):  $12,000 →  OWNER PAY account
TAX (15%):        $3,000  →  TAX account
OPEX (15%):       $3,000  →  OPEX account
INCOME left:      $0
```

The OPEX account becomes your real "operating budget." If something
needs to be paid · it comes from OPEX. If OPEX is empty · the
expense doesn't happen this period.

That's the magic. The cap forces discipline.

---

## The PROFIT account · what to actually DO with it

The PROFIT account is the cash you've earned that's NOT going back
into the business or to you as salary.

**Quarterly · 50% of PROFIT:**
- The owner takes 50% as a quarterly profit distribution
- This is on top of OWNER PAY salary
- This is the reward · the "why we do this"

**Quarterly · 50% of PROFIT stays in PROFIT account:**
- Builds a war chest for opportunities
- Buffer for downturns
- Funds future investments

You should NEVER touch the PROFIT account for operating expenses.
It's not an emergency fund. It's not a slush fund. It's the
quarterly reward.

---

## The TAX account · the discipline saver

Most operators forget about taxes until tax day · then panic. The
TAX account ends that.

15% of every dollar that enters INCOME goes to TAX (adjust for your
jurisdiction).

When tax day comes · the money is already there. No scramble. No
debt. No surprise.

For US solopreneurs running an LLC at $100K-$500K revenue · 15-20%
is the right tax allocation. For higher earners or different
structures · adjust.

---

## The OWNER PAY account · how YOU get paid

Most founders pay themselves last (which means: rarely). Profit
First reverses this. OWNER PAY is taken out FIRST (after profit).

**Why this matters:**
- Forces the business to be profitable enough to pay you
- Removes the "I'll pay myself when there's extra" trap
- Trains the business to support its founder

**Setting your owner pay:**
- Calculate what you actually need for personal expenses
- That's your minimum
- The percentage on your real-revenue is your target

If your minimum personal need exceeds your real-revenue's owner pay
percentage · the business isn't profitable enough yet. Fix the
business · don't underpay yourself.

---

## The OPEX account · the operating cap

Whatever's left after PROFIT, OWNER PAY, TAX is your OPEX. This
funds:
- Software subscriptions
- Contractors / VAs
- Marketing / ads
- Tools
- All other expenses

**Rule:** if it doesn't fit in OPEX · it doesn't happen this period.

This forces:
- Tool consolidation (do you need 12 SaaS subscriptions?)
- Contractor right-sizing
- Marketing efficiency (can't outspend OPEX)
- Negotiated pricing on annual contracts

The cap creates the discipline. The discipline creates the
sustainable business.

## Implementation · the first 90 days

### Month 1 · Set up

- [ ] Open 4 new bank accounts (or money-market accounts) at your
      existing bank · most banks do this for free
- [ ] Label them: PROFIT · OWNER PAY · TAX · OPEX (you already have
      INCOME)
- [ ] Set up INCOME-only deposit address (clients pay only here)
- [ ] Calculate your starting percentages (use the tables above)

### Month 2 · Run small

- [ ] First 10/25 transfer · use 1% PROFIT (build the muscle)
- [ ] Don't touch PROFIT account
- [ ] Tighten OPEX for the first month · feel the cap

### Month 3 · Calibrate

- [ ] Bump PROFIT to 3-5%
- [ ] Adjust OPEX cap based on actual needs
- [ ] First quarterly profit distribution (small · maybe $500-$2K)
- [ ] Celebrate · the reward IS the system

### Month 6+ · Optimize

- [ ] PROFIT to 5-10%
- [ ] OWNER PAY adjusted to actual market salary
- [ ] OPEX bills negotiated (annual contracts at discounts)
- [ ] Tax allocation accurate for actual liability

## Common Profit First mistakes

1. **Skipping the bank account separation** · "I'll just track in a
   spreadsheet." Doesn't work. The friction of separate accounts is
   the discipline.

2. **Touching PROFIT for emergencies** · break this rule once · the
   system is dead.

3. **Setting % too aggressively** · starting at 15% PROFIT will
   crash OpEx. Start at 1% · climb.

4. **Skipping the quarterly distribution** · the distribution is
   the reward · the WHY of the system. Don't skip it · you'll lose
   motivation.

5. **Trying to apply on volatile revenue** · if your revenue varies
   3x month-to-month, smooth it (use a 3-month rolling average for
   percentage application).
