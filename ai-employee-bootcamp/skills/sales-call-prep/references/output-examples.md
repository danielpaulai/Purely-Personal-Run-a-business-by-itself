# Output Examples — The Sales Call Prep + The Proposal Writer
# Purely Personal · by Daniel Paul

---

## SALES CALL PREP EXAMPLES

### PRE-CALL INTELLIGENCE — GOOD VS WEAK

WEAK:
"Prospect is a marketing professional interested in LinkedIn growth."

STRONG:
"Alex came via a LinkedIn DM after engaging with 3 of our posts about the gap between
posting and converting. Her last message said 'this is exactly what I've been trying to
figure out.' She runs a 1-person HR consulting practice, 4 years in, about £8K/month,
grew entirely on referrals. Changed her headline 6 weeks ago — from 'HR Consultant'
to 'I help scale-ups build HR foundations without the headcount.' That shift usually
signals someone who knows their positioning is off. She mentioned trying a content agency
last year that 'just didn't get the space.' She is taking this call because she wants
to understand whether the problem is the content itself or the strategy."

---

### PAIN EXCAVATION SEQUENCE — GOOD VS WEAK

WEAK:
"Q: What are your main challenges with LinkedIn?
Q: What results are you looking for?
Q: What is your budget?"

STRONG:
"Surface: 'Walk me through what LinkedIn looks like for you right now — specifically,
what happens after you post something.'

Real: 'You mentioned the agency didn't get the space. What did that look like in practice?
What were they producing and why didn't it land?'

Root: 'When you think about the fact that you've been at this for 4 years and still feeling
invisible online — what does that feel like? Not the business impact — the personal part.'

Cost: 'If this is the same conversation in 12 months — you're still posting, still getting
nothing back, still wondering whether it's worth it — what does that cost you?
Not just in revenue. In energy. In whether you keep going.'"

---

### OBJECTION SCRIPTS — GOOD VS WEAK

WEAK:
"Price objection: 'I understand your concern about the investment. Our service provides
great value and many clients see strong ROI.'"

STRONG:
"Price objection script:
'That makes sense — and I'd feel the same way if I'd spent money on something like this
before and not seen results. Can I ask: the agency you used last year — what did that cost
you, roughly? [Wait for answer.] Right. So the money isn't the thing. The question is whether
this is different enough to be worth it. What would you need to see to believe that?'
[Let them answer. That answer tells you exactly what evidence to provide.]"

## PROPOSAL EXAMPLES

### SECTION 1 — THE SITUATION

GENERIC (reject):
"Alex, thank you for taking the time to speak with me. Based on our conversation,
I understand that you are looking to improve your LinkedIn presence and generate more leads.
Many service businesses face similar challenges in today's competitive landscape."

SPECIFIC (accept):
"Alex — here is what I heard when we spoke.

You have been in HR consulting for 4 years. You are good at what you do — your clients
stay, they refer, and the word-of-mouth has been enough to build to where you are.

But you have hit a ceiling. Every new client comes from someone who already knows you.
The day that network stops passing you work — and you know it will eventually — you have nothing.

You tried fixing this with content. You hired an agency last year. They produced posts that
were technically correct and completely hollow. Nobody in your niche would recognise them
as yours. You cancelled after 4 months having spent £3,600 and gained nothing.

So you are here. Not because you do not know what to do. Because every time you have
tried to solve this, it has not worked — and you are not sure whether the problem is
the execution or the strategy."

---

### SECTION 3 — THE SOLUTION (outcomes, not deliverables)

GENERIC (reject):
"Our service includes: 4 LinkedIn posts per week, profile optimisation, content strategy,
monthly analytics report, and weekly check-in calls."

SPECIFIC (accept):
"What changes in 90 days:

Your profile stops describing what you do and starts speaking directly to the HR leader
at a scale-up who is about to make their first senior HR hire and does not know where to start.
When they find you — and they will — they will feel like you wrote your entire profile for them.

Your content stops being 'useful HR tips' and starts being the thinking they wish they had
access to before the problem got expensive. Two posts a week. Written in your voice.
From your experience. For one specific type of person.

The first three weeks you will not see results. You will see your profile views increase.
Week four you will notice people you have never met engaging with your content.
By week eight, you will have had at least one inbound message from someone who found you
through a post or a search and said some version of 'this sounds exactly like what I need.'"

### COVER MESSAGE — GOOD VS WEAK

WEAK:
"Dear Alex, please find attached the proposal from our conversation. I hope it captures
everything we discussed. Please let me know if you have any questions. Kind regards."

STRONG:
"Alex —

Tried to capture exactly what you told me — especially the part about the agency not
getting the space, because that's the thing I'd want you to feel the most differently about.

The situation section might feel a bit uncomfortable to read. That's intentional.
If it doesn't land as true, tell me and we'll adjust.

The one thing I want you to sit with: the cost section. That's not a sales tactic —
those are your numbers from our conversation.

Reply here or call me if you have any questions. No pressure either way."
