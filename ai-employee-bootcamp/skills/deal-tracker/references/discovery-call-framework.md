# Discovery Call Framework, The Sales Call Prep
# Purely Personal · by Daniel Paul

---

## THE CORE PRINCIPLE

A discovery call is not a pitch. It is a diagnostic.

Your job is to find out whether you can solve this person's problem better than
any alternative, including doing nothing. If you can, the sale happens naturally.
If you cannot, you save both people time.

The seller who asks the best questions wins, not the seller with the best deck.

---

## THE CALL STRUCTURE

### PHASE 1, RAPPORT AND CONTEXT (minutes 1-5)

Start with a genuine opener, not "tell me about your business."

**Opening questions that work:**
"Before we get into it, what made you take this call? There are 50 things you could be doing right now."
"What's the one thing you want to leave this call knowing?"
"What's been the biggest challenge in your business in the last 90 days, and I mean the real one, not the polished version?"

**Rules for the opening phase:**
- Your goal is to create a safe space for honesty
- Match their energy level, if they're direct, be direct
- Never start with your company story or your pitch
- The first 5 minutes should be 80% them talking

---

### PHASE 2, QUALIFICATION (minutes 5-20)

The BANT+ questions, ordered by how much they matter.

**Priority order:**
1. Pain (N, Need), Is the problem real and urgent?
2. Authority (A), Can this person say yes?
3. Timeline (T), Is there a reason to act now?
4. Budget (B), Can they pay?
5. Fit (+), Should you actually work together?

**Pain questions, the most important:**
"Walk me through what [the problem] looks like day to day for you."
"How long has this been going on?"
"What have you tried already?"
"Why didn't [previous solution] work?"
"What happens if this is still the same situation in 12 months?"
"What does solving this look like, concretely?"

**Authority questions:**
"When you say 'we', who else is involved in a decision like this?"
"If we found the right fit today, what would the next steps look like on your end?"
"Is there anyone else who would need to be part of this conversation?"

**Timeline questions:**
"Is there a specific date this needs to be solved by?"
"What's driving the urgency right now?"
"What changes if this isn't sorted by [timeframe]?"

**Budget questions (never lead with these):**
"Have you worked with someone on this before? What kind of investment was that?"
"What have you set aside to solve this problem?"
"What's the cost of this problem per month right now?" [Then position your price against that]

**Fit questions:**
"What does success look like for you in 90 days?"
"What do you need from the person you work with to actually get results?"
"What would make this NOT work, from your side?"

---

### PHASE 3, PAIN EXCAVATION (minutes 15-25)

Move through three levels. Most sales conversations stop at the surface.

**THE 3-LEVEL EXCAVATION:**

**Level 1, Surface Pain (what they say in a meeting):**
"So [the specific problem], can you give me a specific recent example?"
Let them describe it. Do not interpret it yet.

**Level 2, Real Pain (what they say to a trusted colleague):**
"That sounds frustrating. What's the actual impact of that on [the business / team / pipeline]?"
"How does that show up day to day?"
"If you had to put a number on what this is costing you, what would it be?"

**Level 3, Root Pain (the identity cost):**
"I'm curious, when this problem keeps coming back, what does that feel like for you personally?"
"What's the most frustrating part, not the business impact, the personal part?"
"You've been dealing with this for [timeframe]. What's kept it from being solved until now?"

**Why this matters:**
When you close at the Root Pain level, the price becomes almost irrelevant.
People will pay almost anything to stop feeling the identity threat.
People will defer and delay for almost anything that only solves the Surface Pain.

### PHASE 4, OBJECTION HANDLING

The 6 objection categories and how to handle each.

**PRICE OBJECTION**
Stated: "It's too expensive."
Real: "I'm not convinced the return justifies the risk."

Wrong response: "We can do payment plans" / "It's an investment" / Immediate discount
Right response: "I hear you on price, let me make sure I understand the picture.
You mentioned this problem is costing you [X] per month. If we solve it in [timeframe],
the investment is [Y]. Does the math work, or is the uncertainty about whether it'll work?"

[Get specific about ROI before defending price]

---

**TIME OBJECTION**
Stated: "I'm too busy right now / maybe next quarter."
Real: "I don't believe I can implement this without it being hard."

Wrong response: "It won't take much of your time"
Right response: "Totally understand. Can I ask, is it the time to make it work, or the time
to figure out if it's worth making work? Because those are different problems."

[Get specific about what 'too busy' actually means in their situation]

---

**TRUST OBJECTION**
Stated: "I've been burned before / I need to do more research."
Real: "I don't yet believe you can deliver for someone like me specifically."

Wrong response: References and case studies immediately
Right response: "That's completely fair, and I'd rather earn it than ask for it.
What would I need to show you specifically for this to feel like the right call?
Because 'more research' usually means something specific."

[Find the specific gap in their trust and address that, not all trust objections]

---

**AUTHORITY OBJECTION**
Stated: "I need to check with my partner / team."
Real: "I want cover if this doesn't work out."

Wrong response: "Let's get everyone on a call"
Right response: "Of course, that makes sense. What do you think they'll want to know?
Because I'd rather prepare you to have that conversation well than just meet with everyone
again from scratch."

[Help them sell it internally rather than adding more meetings]

---

**NEED OBJECTION**
Stated: "I'm not sure this is what I need right now."
Real: "I can't clearly see the specific outcome I'm paying for."

Wrong response: Explaining the offer more thoroughly
Right response: "What does the right solution look like for you? I want to make sure 
I understand what you actually need, not just what I offer."

[Reground in their specific situation, not your general offering]

---

**URGENCY OBJECTION**
Stated: "Let me come back to this in a few months."
Real: "The pain hasn't hit the threshold where action feels necessary."

Wrong response: "The price goes up in [timeframe]" (fake urgency)
Right response: "That's fair. Can I just ask, what would need to change in the next few months
for this to feel more urgent? Because from what you've described, [the cost of waiting]
seems like it compounds rather than resolves."

[Make the cost of inaction visible, never create fake urgency]

---

### PHASE 5, TRANSITION TO NEXT STEP (minutes 25-30)

Never leave a call without a clear next step.

**The 4 possible next steps:**
1. You will send a proposal by [specific date]
2. They will speak to [partner/team] and get back to you by [specific date]
3. You are not a fit, and you tell them clearly
4. They are ready to start, take the card / send the contract

**How to close the call:**
"Based on everything we've talked about, I think there's a real fit here.
What I'd like to do is [specific next step] by [specific date].
Does that work on your end?"

**If they are not ready:**
"I want to be honest with you, based on what you've shared, I think [X] before [Y] would
set you up better. Here is what I would suggest doing first."

[Being honest about fit, even when it means not selling, is what builds the referral network]

---

## RED FLAGS, STOP AND CLARIFY IF YOU HEAR THESE

→ "We just need a quote to compare options", they are collecting data, not buying
→ "Our budget is already allocated", you are too late in their cycle
→ "We tried something similar last year", find out why it failed before going further
→ "This needs to go through a procurement process", understand the timeline before investing
→ "Can you send a deck I can share internally?", find out who the real decision-maker is first
→ "We're planning to do this ourselves", find out if they have tried and failed first
