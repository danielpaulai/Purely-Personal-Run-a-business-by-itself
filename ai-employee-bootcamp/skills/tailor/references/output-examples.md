# Output Examples
# Tailor Reference — Purely Personal Bootcamp

Before/after examples for each of the 5 skills.
Use these to calibrate quality. If your output looks like the GENERIC examples — rebuild it.
If it looks like the FITTED examples — it's ready to deliver.

These examples use a fictional participant: Maya Chen, a fractional CFO who helps
Series A SaaS founders get finance-ready before their Series B raise.
Her ICP: SaaS founders, 12-24 months post-Series A, $1-3M ARR, first-time fundraisers.
Her offer: The Series B Readiness Sprint — 90-day engagement, £18K.
Her competitors: generic fractional CFO networks, Big 4 advisory arms.
Her top proof point: "Helped 3 clients close Series B rounds totalling £47M in 2024."

### CONTENT PILLAR — FITTED (accept)

```
PILLAR 1: THE SERIES B BLIND SPOT
──────────────────────────────────
What this pillar owns:
The specific finance decisions Series A founders make in months 12-24 that make
Series B investors say no — and that no fractional CFO network ever talks about
because they're focused on month-end close, not fundraise readiness.

Content intent: Build trust + Generate leads

Why Maya can own it:
She's sat in 40+ Series B data rooms. She knows exactly which revenue recognition
issues, cash flow narratives, and unit economics presentations kill term sheets.
Her competitors talk about "financial discipline." She talks about what actually
killed the round.

What competitors are doing:
Generic fractional CFO content focuses on "building strong financial foundations."
That's table stakes. Nobody is writing about the specific patterns that make
Series B investors nervous — because most fractional CFOs have never been in
those conversations.

Research finding that informed this pillar:
From SaaS founder communities: "My Series B fell through and I still don't know
exactly why" appears in multiple founder forums. The gap between what they're told
("metrics look good") and what killed the deal is the pillar.

3 post ideas to start immediately:
1. "Your Series A investor said your metrics look great. Your Series B investor passed.
   Here are the 3 things they saw that nobody told you about." (Warning hook / PAS)
2. "I've been in 40 Series B data rooms. These 4 slides kill more deals than bad metrics."
   (Stat hook / Case Study framework — Maya's direct experience)
3. "The fractional CFO said your books were clean. The investor still passed.
   Here's the difference between clean books and fundraise-ready books." (Contrarian / BAB)
```

## SKILL 2 — LINKEDIN CAPTION WRITER

### VOICE LOCK — GENERIC (reject)

```
## VOICE DNA CHECK

Is a Voice DNA or voice profile provided?
- YES → apply their voice throughout.
- NO → write in Daniel Paul's default voice.
```

### VOICE LOCK — FITTED (accept)

```
## VOICE LOCK

This skill writes in Maya Chen's voice. Always. No fallback.

Voice rules (non-negotiable):
1. Numbers anchor every claim. "Several clients" → "3 clients." "Strong revenue" → "£47M raised."
   If there's no number, find one or cut the claim.
2. Write for one specific person: a SaaS founder, 14 months post-Series A, reading this
   at 11pm wondering if their numbers are Series B-ready. Not "founders" or "executives."
3. Contrarian positions only. The niche is saturated with "build strong financial foundations."
   Maya writes what investors see that founders don't. If the post could come from any
   fractional CFO — it doesn't come from Maya.
4. Short sentences for verdicts. Longer sentences for context.
   "Your books are clean. Your fundraise narrative isn't. Those are different problems."
5. CTAs always name the action, the outcome, and what they get:
   "DM me SERIES B and I'll send the 8-question fundraise readiness audit."

Banned words (remove before delivery):
leverage / optimize / strategic / holistic / robust / scalable / thought leader /
empower / foster / ecosystem / navigate / cutting-edge / transformative / unlock /
in today's landscape / it's important to note / game-changer / deep dive

Sentence rhythm: Short verdict. Medium context. Short punch. One idea per paragraph.
Energy level: 3/5 — Direct and precise. No hype. No enthusiasm markers.
CTA format: "DM me [KEYWORD] and I'll [SPECIFIC THING]" — always.

If any output violates these rules — rewrite before delivering.
```

```
60 seconds — that's the one to fix before the process starts.
```

```
REAL: They're not sure about ROI.
```

### OBJECTION SCRIPT — FITTED (accept)

```
PRICE OBJECTION (most likely for Maya's ICP at this stage):
STATED: "£18K is more than I budgeted for fractional CFO support."
REAL: "I've never paid this for finance advice before. I'm not sure
       what 'fundraise-ready' actually means in practice — and whether
       it's different from what my current accountant does."
RESPONSE: "That's fair — and I want to make sure I'm not selling you
something your accountant is already doing. Can I ask: what did your
accountant say when you asked them to run through your Series B data
room readiness? [Wait for answer.]
That answer usually tells us whether we're solving the same problem."
MOVE: If they say "they haven't done that" — explain exactly what the
sprint covers and why it's different from compliance-focused accounting.
If they say "they did run through it" — ask what they found and probe
whether it was fundraise-specific or general financial review.
Never defend the price before establishing the gap.
```

## QUALITY TEST: HOW TO TELL GENERIC FROM FITTED

Ask these 4 questions about any output section:

1. **Could a competitor produce this exact section?**
   Generic: yes. Fitted: no — it references Maya's specific proof, ICP, or angle.

2. **Does it name a specific number, person, timeframe, or situation?**
   Generic: no, or vague ("several clients", "strong results"). Fitted: yes, always.

3. **Would Maya read this and say "I wrote that"?**
   Generic: maybe. Fitted: yes — because it uses her rhythm, her specificity standard,
   her banned words list, and her CTA format.

4. **Does it reference the ICP's exact situation or pain in their own words?**
   Generic: it describes the ICP. Fitted: it speaks their language — the words they'd
   use in a frustrated Monday morning conversation with a peer.

All four yes = deliver.
Any no = find which element failed and rebuild that section only.
