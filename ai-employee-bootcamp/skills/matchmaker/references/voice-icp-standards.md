# Voice & ICP Language Standards
# Matchmaker Reference — Purely Personal Bootcamp

This file teaches the Matchmaker how to evaluate voice accuracy and ICP language
precision in any skill. Read this before auditing any skill.

---

## WHAT VOICE ACTUALLY IS

Voice is not "friendly" or "professional" or "conversational."
Those are adjectives anyone could use to describe anyone's writing.

Voice is a set of specific, measurable patterns that appear consistently across
a person's output and make their writing immediately recognisable.

A skill that doesn't have precise voice rules will drift toward AI-average:
polished, structured, and indistinguishable from a million other outputs.

---

## THE 8 DIMENSIONS OF VOICE — AUDIT EACH ONE

When auditing a skill against the participant's Voice DNA, check all eight dimensions.
Each missing dimension is a gap the Tailor must fill.

### 1. Sentence Rhythm
How long are their sentences on average?
Short punchy sentences signal confidence and authority.
Longer sentences with embedded clauses signal thinking-in-public and nuance.
Most voices use a signature pattern — short-short-medium, or medium with short punches.

**What to look for in the Voice DNA:**
Do they tend toward short declaratives? Long compound sentences? Questions as punctuation?
A skill must mirror this pattern or it won't sound like the person.

### 2. Energy Level
How much intensity does this person bring to their writing?
Scale: 1 (calm, measured, academic) → 5 (high energy, punchy, exclamation-heavy)

**What to look for:**
Does the Voice DNA describe them as direct? Warm? Fierce? Dry? Understated?
A skill writing at energy level 4 for a person who writes at energy level 2 is wrong.

### 3. Vocabulary Range
Do they use simple, everyday words — or precise, elevated, industry-specific language?
Do they use slang? Colloquialisms? Industry jargon deliberately (to signal belonging)
or do they avoid it (to differentiate)?

**What to look for:**
Are there specific words or phrases that appear repeatedly in their posts?
Are there words they've explicitly banned because they sound like everyone else?

### 4. Structural Patterns
Do they write in lists or prose?
Do they use line breaks aggressively (one sentence per line)?
Do they use em-dashes, ellipses, or brackets as structural tools?
Do they open with a one-liner hook or a scene-setting statement?

**What to look for:**
The structure IS the voice. A person who writes one-sentence-per-line on LinkedIn
sounds completely different in a skill that writes dense paragraphs.

### 5. Emotional Register
What emotion do they lead with? What emotion do they leave the reader with?
Options: curiosity, conviction, empathy, provocation, humour, vulnerability, authority.

**What to look for:**
A skill must know what emotional state the person wants to create.
"Warm and motivational" requires completely different language than "dry and contrarian."

### 6. Signature Phrases & Recurring Constructs
Most people have 3-5 phrases or sentence structures they return to again and again.
These are the fingerprints of their voice.
A good skill knows these and uses them at the right moments.

**What to look for in Voice DNA:**
Are there specific phrases they use? Specific ways they open a point?
Specific ways they close a loop or deliver a punchline?

### 7. Banned Words & Phrases
Every strong voice has things it never says — because those things are what everyone else says.
A skill without a banned list will default to the AI average every time.

Common banned categories:
- Overused LinkedIn phrases: "game-changer," "deep dive," "unpack," "at the end of the day"
- Generic AI output words: "delve," "crucial," "leverage," "foster," "navigate"
- Industry jargon the niche overuses: varies by person and market

**What to look for:**
Does the Voice DNA have an explicit banned list?
If not, extract what they're implicitly avoiding based on their actual writing samples.

### 8. CTA Style
How does this person ask for action?
Direct ("DM me the word X") vs soft ("let me know if this resonates") vs
question-based ("which of these is your biggest issue?").

**What to look for:**
A skill writing hard CTAs for a person whose voice is warm and conversational
will feel off-brand on every single output.

## ICP LANGUAGE STANDARDS

The most common gap across all starter skills: they describe the ICP instead of
speaking the ICP's language.

"Founders who want to grow their LinkedIn presence" is a description.
"Bootstrapped founders who keep posting and getting silence and are starting to
wonder if LinkedIn is just for people with big followings already" is ICP language.

The difference: the second version uses the words the ICP uses in their own head
when they're feeling the problem. Not a paraphrase. The actual language.

### How to extract ICP language from foundation documents

When reading the ICP document, look for:

**Direct quotes from clients or prospects:**
Any place the participant has written down what clients actually said to them.
These are gold — use them verbatim, not paraphrased.

**Problem descriptions:**
How does the ICP describe their problem before they know your solution exists?
A skill must use this language — not a polished marketing version of it.

**Desired outcome descriptions:**
What does the ICP say they want? In their exact words?
Not "to grow their business" — what specific version of success do they describe?

**Objections:**
What does the ICP say when they're not ready to buy?
These are the objections every skill must be equipped to handle or pre-empt.

## THE INVISIBILITY DIAGNOSTIC — VOICE CHECKLIST

Use this to score whether a skill's output will pass or fail in the participant's voice.
A skill that consistently fails this diagnostic needs voice work.

**V — Voice:** Does it sound like a specific person or like anyone?
One marker: remove the name. Could this post belong to three different people you know?
If yes → voice gap.

**P — POV:** Does it take a clear position someone could disagree with?
Generic content states facts. Voiced content takes stances.
"LinkedIn is a great platform for B2B" = no POV.
"LinkedIn rewards consistency over virality and most people quit before the compound effect kicks in" = POV.

**S — Specificity:** Does it name specific things — numbers, people, timeframes, situations?
"Several clients" = not specific. "Three clients in the past 6 weeks" = specific.
A skill without access to proof points will always fail specificity.

**A — Asking:** Does it drive one clear action?
Not three options. Not a vague "let me know what you think."
One ask. One CTA. One next step.
A skill without the participant's preferred CTA style will default to the wrong one.

## HOW TO SCORE A VOICE GAP

Minor voice gap: The skill has some voice rules but is missing 1-2 dimensions.
Output will be recognisable but slightly flat or mismatched in places.

Major voice gap: The skill has no voice rules at all or is missing 4+ dimensions.
Output will sound like AI-written content in someone else's name.
Every single piece will need heavy editing before posting.

Critical voice gap: The skill has rules that actively contradict the Voice DNA.
For example: the skill writes in formal prose for someone who writes in
aggressive one-liners. The output is not just weak — it damages the brand.
