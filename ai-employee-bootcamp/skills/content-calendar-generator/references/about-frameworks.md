# About Section Frameworks, Purely Personal Reference

## What the About Section Actually Does

The About section is NOT a bio. It is a conversion page. Its job is to take someone who is curious and turn them into someone who takes action.

LinkedIn gives you 2,600 characters. The best About sections use roughly 800 to 1,400.

The first 300 characters matter most, that is what shows before "see more" on mobile.

---

## The 3 Real-World Structures That Convert (from top creators)

### Structure 1: The Pain-Agitate-Bridge (Hatice Kamran)

Step 1, Name the desire (3 short questions the ICP is asking themselves):
"Want to create content that gets you sales?
Want to grow your personal brand on LinkedIn?
Want to grow your business with organic content?"

Step 2, Agitate the current reality (name what is blocking them):
"Your brand doesn't match your real-world impact.
Your LinkedIn isn't working and you feel stuck."

Step 3, Introduce the harsh truth:
"Without the right strategy, you'll waste even more time."

Step 4, Bridge to the solution (you, and how you help):
"I've helped top-tier entrepreneurs build powerful brands and I can help you, too."

Step 5, Specific proof:
"That's exactly how I built my brand from 0 to 27,000+ followers in only 15 months."

Step 6, One clear CTA:
"Book a discovery call now (using the first link in the featured section)."

---

### Structure 2: The Story-Proof-System (Lara Acosta)

Step 1, Bold opening statement that challenges a common belief:
"Everyone wants to build a personal brand, and I figured out how."

Step 2, Immediate proof with specific numbers:
"With over 20M+ impressions, 300,000 followers across socials, and multiple 6 figures made..."

Step 3, Origin story using a timeline format:
"In 2022: I was unemployed and trying to get into a master's degree.
In 2023: I became the #1 female creator on LinkedIn.
In 2024: I'm building the best education platform."

Step 4, What you do for others:
"Helped, coached and worked with 500+ to build content systems, increase engagement, improve their writing."

Step 5, Multi-option CTA (works when you have multiple entry points):
"1. Subscribe to my YouTube.
2. Join 15,100+ people in my newsletter.
3. Join 500+ members inside Literallyacademy.com"

Step 6, Warm closing that welcomes all readers:
"See you on the feed."

---

### Structure 3: The Narrative Flow (Charlie Hills / Daniel Paul style)

Step 1, Contrarian or curiosity-driven opener:
"Are you posting on LinkedIn and getting nothing back?"

Step 2, Mirror the reader's specific situation in their exact language.

Step 3, Position yourself as the bridge between where they are and where they want to be.

Step 4, Proof woven naturally into the narrative (not a separate list).

Step 5, Method briefly explained (what working with you looks like).

Step 6, Single CTA that requires zero thinking.

## 14 Best Practices (from LinkedIn Talent Blog + Creator Analysis)

1. Passion and proof, open with what drives you AND a number that makes it real.
2. Explain your current role clearly, what do you do, for whom, what problem does it solve.
3. Frame your career, if the path is non-linear, connect the dots so they make sense.
4. Highlight genuine achievements, be specific. The greatest compliment I ever got at work was... beats "I have experience in..."
5. Reveal your character, gratitude, humility, authenticity build trust faster than credentials alone.
6. Show life outside work, one sentence showing you're a human being, not just a professional.
7. Ask for what you want, don't make people guess. Name the one action you want them to take.
8. Add rich media where possible, link to work that shows rather than tells.
9. Make your first sentence count, it is what shows in search results before "see more."
10. Pump in the keywords, your ICP's search language should appear naturally throughout.
11. Cut the jargon, "strategic," "results-oriented," "motivated self-starter" mean nothing. Remove them all.
12. Write how you speak, read it aloud. If it sounds stiff, rewrite it.
13. Use white space, short paragraphs. Never a wall of text.
14. Keep the CTA to one instruction, multiple options create paralysis.

## Opening Lines That Work vs Ones That Don't

WORKS:
"Everyone wants to build a personal brand, and I figured out how." (challenges a belief, hints at a secret)
"Most content experts have never sold a thing." (contrarian, stops scrolling)
"Want to create content that gets you sales?" (speaks directly to the ICP's desire)
"Are you posting on LinkedIn and getting nothing back?" (mirrors the reader's frustration)
"I left my job to start an agency. I didn't expect much. Now I help 200+ founders." (origin + proof)

DOESN'T WORK:
"I am a passionate marketing professional with 10 years of experience." (boring, generic)
"Hi, I'm [Name] and I help businesses grow." (vague, applies to everyone)
"Welcome to my profile!" (wastes the most valuable space on the page)
"Throughout my career, I have developed expertise in..." (immediate red flag)

## CTA Formats That Convert (ranked best to worst)

1. "DM me [KEYWORD] and I'll [specific thing].", lowest friction, highest conversion
2. "Book a free [specific call type] at [link].", works when the outcome of the call is clear
3. "Join [X] people in [newsletter/community] at [link].", social proof makes it easier to say yes
4. "Subscribe to [specific named thing] here: [link].", works when the thing has a known reputation
5. "Let's connect!", weakest, no direction, no reason to act

---

## What Never to Include in an About Section

Bullet points as the primary structure (they feel like a resume, not a conversation)
A list of job duties without outcomes
Vague statements like "I'm passionate about making a difference"
More than one CTA (pick one, make it frictionless)
Your phone number or personal email (unprofessional, spam risk)
Every job you've ever had (that's what the Experience section is for)
